## 1. Network History

### Evolution Timeline

- **1960s**: ARPANET developed by ARPA (Advanced Research Projects Agency), U.S. Department of Defense. First operational packet-switching network, connecting four nodes (UCLA, Stanford, UCSB, University of Utah) in 1969.
- **1970s**: Development of Ethernet by Robert Metcalfe at Xerox PARC (1973). TCP/IP protocol suite designed by Vint Cerf and Bob Kahn.
- **1980s**: ARPANET adopted TCP/IP (1983), marking the true birth of the modern Internet. Domain Name System (DNS) introduced (1984).
- **1990s**: World Wide Web invented by Tim Berners-Lee (1989-1991) at CERN. Commercialization of the Internet begins.
- **2000s onward**: Broadband, wireless networks, mobile networks, cloud computing, IoT.

### Key Definition

> "A computer network is a collection of interconnected autonomous computing devices that can exchange information and share resources."

### Exam Points

- ARPANET is considered the predecessor of the modern Internet.
- The concept of packet switching (proposed by Paul Baran and Donald Davies independently) replaced circuit switching for data communication.
- NSFNET (1986) later expanded ARPANET's role, connecting universities and research institutions.

## 2. Introduction to Computer Network

### What is a Computer Network

A computer network is an interconnection of two or more computing devices (nodes) connected through a communication medium (wired or wireless) that allows sharing of data, resources, and services.

### Basic Components of a Network

| Component | Description |
|---|---|
| Node | Any device connected to the network (computer, printer, server, etc.) |
| Link | Communication channel connecting two nodes (wired/wireless) |
| Protocol | Set of rules governing data communication |
| NIC (Network Interface Card) | Hardware that enables a device to connect to a network |
| Server | A device/software that provides services or resources to other devices (clients) |

### Why Networks are Needed

- Enable communication between devices located at different places.
- Allow centralized management and control of resources.
- Reduce cost by sharing hardware/software resources.
- Enable faster and more reliable data transfer.

## 3. Uses of Computer Network

### Business Applications

- **Resource Sharing**: Sharing printers, storage, applications across an organization, reducing hardware cost.
- **Information Sharing**: Centralized databases accessible by authorized users from any location.
- **Communication**: Email, video conferencing, instant messaging for internal and external communication.
- **E-commerce**: Business transactions (B2B, B2C) conducted over the network.
- **Client-Server Model**: Businesses run applications on servers, accessed by multiple client machines.

### Home/Personal Applications

- Access to remote information (browsing, online banking, e-shopping).
- Person-to-person communication (social media, messaging, video calls).
- Interactive entertainment (online gaming, streaming).
- Electronic commerce (online shopping, digital payments).

### Mobile Users

- Access to the Internet from anywhere using mobile networks.
- Use of applications such as GPS navigation, mobile banking, and cloud-based services.

### Exam Points

- Networks are broadly used for three purposes: resource sharing, communication, and remote access to information.
- Cost reduction through sharing of expensive resources (printers, servers, licenses) is a major driving factor in business use.

## 5. Types of Network

Networks are classified based on **geographical area of coverage**.

### 5.1 LAN (Local Area Network)

- Covers a small geographical area: a single building, office, or campus.
- Ownership: typically privately owned by a single organization.
- Speed: high data transfer rate (100 Mbps to several Gbps).
- Example: A network connecting computers within a college lab or office.

### 5.2 MAN (Metropolitan Area Network)

- Covers a city or a large campus spread across a metropolitan area.
- Typically formed by interconnecting multiple LANs.
- Example: Cable TV networks, city-wide networks connecting government offices.

### 5.3 WAN (Wide Area Network)

- Covers a large geographical area: country, continent, or the entire globe.
- Ownership: often owned by multiple organizations or telecom operators; uses leased communication lines.
- Speed: comparatively lower than LAN due to larger distances and infrastructure involved.
- Example: The Internet is the largest example of a WAN.

### Comparison Table (Exam Important)

| Parameter | LAN | MAN | WAN |
|---|---|---|---|
| Full Form | Local Area Network | Metropolitan Area Network | Wide Area Network |
| Coverage Area | Building/Campus | City | Country/Globe |
| Ownership | Single organization | One or more organizations | Multiple organizations/ISPs |
| Data Rate | Very High | Moderate | Comparatively Low |
| Setup Cost | Low | Moderate | High |
| Maintenance | Easy | Moderate | Difficult |
| Example | Office/College LAN | Cable TV network | The Internet |
| Fault Tolerance | High | Moderate | Low (due to distributed infra) |

### Exam Points

- LAN is privately owned; WAN often uses public/leased infrastructure.
- MAN sits between LAN and WAN in terms of scale.
- Data transmission speed decreases as geographical coverage increases (LAN > MAN > WAN).
